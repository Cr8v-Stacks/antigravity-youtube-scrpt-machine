# Script

You think modern courtroom drama is wild? Then let me take you back to the time the Catholic Church put a corpse on trial.

In the year 897, Pope Stephen the Sixth decided he hated his predecessor, Pope Formosus.

The only problem? Formosus had been dead for nine months.

But Stephen didn’t let a minor detail like death stop justice. He had the rotting corpse dug up, dressed in full papal robes, and propped up on a throne to face trial.

The charges? Violating church rules to get promoted, which was pretty rich coming from a guy holding court with a skeleton.

This was the Cadaver Synod. A young, terrified deacon stood next to the corpse, screaming out defense answers on its behalf. The corpse stayed silent.

Unsurprisingly, it lost. The court declared Formosus' entire papacy invalid. They stripped the body, chopped off the three fingers he used for blessings, and threw him into the River Tiber.

But the story gets better. The public was so disgusted, they rippled into a mob, threw Stephen into prison, and strangled him. So, the next time you get a parking ticket, just remember, at least they didn't dig you up to argue it.

And at least the judge won't end up strangled. Yet.

*   **Key Facts**:
    *   The trial occurred in Rome in January 897.
    *   Formosus was accused of perjury and violating canon law.
    *   His fingers of benediction (blessing fingers) were severed, and he was thrown in the Tiber.
    *   Stephen VI was deposed, imprisoned, and strangled in August 897.
*   **Misconception Map**:
    *   *Folk answer*: Court systems have always been strictly logical and respect the deceased.
    *   *Correction*: Power struggles in the medieval papacy led to literal physical trials of corpses to delegitimize past political acts.
