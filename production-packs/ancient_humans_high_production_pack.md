# Script

You probably have a picture in your head right now of ancient history as a completely sober, clean-cut documentary. You picture historical figures spending their entire lives speaking in deep monotone voices, building pristine marble columns, and wearing perfectly ironed white togas. Or, at the other extreme, you imagine a pack of wild cave dwellers accidentally chewing on a strange forest root, screaming at a cloud, and unlocking the entire concept of religion by complete accident. But both of these mental pictures are completely wrong. The truth is much more organized, surprisingly bureaucratic, and slightly more chaotic than you think. Throughout our long prehistory, getting high wasn't a modern glitch, a rebellion, or a rare accident. It was a core feature of early human societies, woven into the fabric of daily life.

To find the real origin of this behavior, we have to travel back seventy thousand years to Shanidar Cave in modern-day Iraq. When archaeologists uncovered a Neanderthal skeleton buried deep in the cave, they found it surrounded by dense clumps of ancient flower pollen. For decades, schoolbooks told a sweet, sentimental story about Neanderthals placing lovely flowers on the graves of their loved ones. But when modern botanists analyzed the specific pollen, they didn't find roses. They found yarrow, chamomile, and ephedra—a plant containing a powerful, heart-racing stimulant. These ancient humans weren't just decorating graves with pretty colors. They were systematically harvesting active botanical chemicals, using plants to alter their biology and modify their conscious states long before civilization existed.

Fast forward to ancient Egypt, around four thousand years ago. If you look closely at the limestone tomb walls, you will notice carvings of high-status Egyptians sniffing the beautiful blue water lily, known as the blue lotus. This wasn't a simple aesthetic preference or a nice perfume. The blue lotus contains apomorphine and nuciferine, psychoactive compounds that induce a calm, hypnotic euphoria. But the Egyptians didn't just stumble upon this weed. They developed a massive agricultural system to cultivate it. They systematically harvested the flowers and infused them into large clay jars of red wine. Crucially, this wasn't a free-for-all party. The temple priests controlled the supply, using the euphoric brew to convince followers that royalty possessed divine power.

If you think the ancient Greeks were all cool logic, geometry, and dry political philosophy, you are ignoring the most famous night of the Athenian year. Every autumn, the elite of Athens walked fourteen miles to the sanctuary of Eleusis to participate in the Eleusinian Mysteries. Inside a dark, windowless hall called the Telesterion, they drank a secret ritual beverage called Kykeon. For two thousand years, revealing the ingredients of this brew was a crime punishable by immediate death. But modern toxicologists have identified the likely secret: ergot. This toxic fungus grows on barley and contains chemical precursors to LSD. The temple priests were systematically brewing ergot-infected barley to trigger profound collective hallucinations, using chemical terror to forge absolute political unity.

Meanwhile, in the freezing Siberian tundra, indigenous tribes discovered a different chemical shortcut: the fly agaric mushroom. This iconic red-and-white mushroom is highly psychoactive, but it comes with a major problem: it contains toxins that make humans violently sick. The tribes quickly noticed that reindeer could eat the mushrooms without vomiting. More importantly, they realized the active hallucinogen passes through the kidneys unchanged, while the toxic compounds are filtered out. So, to get the trip without the sickness, followers drank the urine of reindeer that had eaten the mushrooms. Shamans even drank the urine of other shamans. This became a structured economic system, where reindeer were traded for dried mushrooms, and access to chemical output was a high-status commodity.

Across the Atlantic in Mesoamerica, the Aztecs took the system to an even higher level of state control. They worshipped Xochipilli, the Prince of Flowers, a god covered in carvings of psychoactive plants like mushrooms, tobacco, and morning glory. During royal coronations and sacred calendar festivals, the Aztec elite consumed teonanácatl, meaning the flesh of the gods—a species of psilocybin mushroom. They didn't just chew them raw; they served them in elegant clay cups mixed with honey and cold, frothy chocolate to mask the bitter taste. But the state kept a tight grip on these experiences. Consuming the sacred mushrooms without royal permission was a capital offense. The state used the mind-altering mushrooms to reinforce the social hierarchy, ensuring only the ruling class had direct access to the divine.

But we don't even have to look at exotic hallucinogens to see how altered states built our world. We just have to look at beer. Some anthropologists argue that early humans didn't start farming wheat to make bread; they did it to secure a steady supply of alcohol. Bread is difficult to bake and spoils quickly, but fermenting grain into beer kills pathogens and preserves calories. In ancient Mesopotamia, workers were paid in daily rations of beer. The temple complex acted as a massive state-run brewery, distributing liquid wages to mobilize thousands of laborers for monument construction. Beer wasn't a reward for building the pyramids and temples; it was the biological fuel that made the logistics of early states possible.

Across every continent, we see the same repeating pattern: ancient humans did not consume psychoactive substances in a vacuum of wild, chaotic freedom. Every society that discovered a chemical shortcut immediately wrapped it in rules, rituals, and strict gatekeeping. They knew that raw, unregulated access to mind-altering plants would lead to complete social instability. Shamanic licensing, sacred cups, secret languages, and temple monopolies were designed to prevent chaotic abuse and channel the experience into social cohesion. If everyone could trip whenever they wanted, the social hierarchy would collapse. The priests and rulers knew that controlling the gate to altered consciousness was the ultimate form of political power. They turned chemical shortcuts into institutions, using rituals to reinforce tribal obedience.

So, did ancient humans get high? Absolutely. But they didn't do it to drop out of society. They did it to hold society together. Our modern war on drugs, the multi-billion-dollar pharmaceutical industry, and the complex regulations around alcohol and cannabis aren't modern inventions. They are just the latest version of a seventy-thousand-year-old loop. Humans will always seek chemical shortcuts to alter their biology, and institutions will always step in to restrict, license, and tax those shortcuts. We didn't invent getting high. We just legalized the bureaucracy around it. The next time you look at a modern pharmacy shelf or buy a bottle of medicine, remember that the Neanderthals started the very same search in a cave.

We examine the systemic incentive loops that drove ancient authorities to regulate and monopolize chemical shortcuts, revealing that our modern drug wars, pharmaceutical regulations, and licensing systems are just the latest iterations of a 70,000-year-old cycle of human desire and institutional control.
