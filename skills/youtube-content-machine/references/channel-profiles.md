# Channel Profiles

Use these as priority examples and active operating profiles. Do not treat them as the only channels this skill can support.

## Human Behavior / System-Explanation Channels

### Positioning

Use this profile for channels that explain why humans behave the way they do through origins, effects, invisible systems, incentives, and simple visual models.

Useful short positioning:

- Why humans behave the way they do, explained simply.
- Human behavior explained through origins, effects, and invisible systems.
- A visual encyclopedia of human behavior using simple system models.

This is not self-help, motivation, generic psychology facts, or abstract theory for its own sake. It should feel like reality explained as a simple system diagram.

### Strong Content Lanes

- Human origin questions: "Why do humans...", "What did ancient humans..."
- System curiology: hidden loops behind status, money, social media, inequality, decisions.
- Weird human effects: named psychology effects and biases.
- What-if human behavior simulations.

### Winning Formats

- "Why do humans..." videos.
- "What did humans..." videos.
- "The ___ Effect" videos.
- "What if humans..." videos.
- "The system that..." videos, when the title remains understandable.

### Title Rule

Titles should feel like a question about human nature, a strange truth, or a named effect. Avoid abstract system jargon in the title even if the video uses systems thinking inside.

Better:

- Why Humans Are Obsessed With Status.
- Why Do Humans Follow Crowds?
- What Did Ancient Humans Do All Day?
- The Effect That Explains Why People Copy Each Other.

Weaker:

- The Incentive Loop Of Modern Society.
- Systemic Status Behavior Model.

### Script Shape

For standard long-form human-behavior/system-explanation videos, usually 8:00-9:30 unless the research and outline honestly support a longer runtime:

1. Hook: attack the viewer's likely wrong answer, then open the deeper question.
2. Setup: why the behavior matters.
3. Simple human model: 2-3 layers.
4. System reveal: hidden loop, incentive, or evolutionary explanation.
5. Modern manifestation: social media, money, work, dating, status, attention, or culture.
6. Final twist: a reflective or unsettling conclusion.

For "What did humans..." or origin-question videos, do not merely restate the title question. Identify the answer viewers probably brought with them, break it fast, then show the older or stranger system underneath.

### Visual Identity

Use a layered visual language:

- Editorial diagram humans as the primary identity.
- Notebook sketch style for deep thinking and origin explanations.
- Minimal expressive cartoon humans for emotional/social moments.

Avoid default stick-figure explainer and generic whiteboard style unless the user specifically wants them.

### Visual Identity Lock

Default episode visual system: Editorial Diagram System for most behavior/system videos.

Identity preset name: Human behavior loop identity.

Approved alternates and inserts:

- Social Comic System for social pressure, embarrassment, status, group reaction, and dialogue moments.
- Notebook Sketch System for reflective theory, origin thinking, and deep system breakdowns.
- Hybrid Sketch System Map for "What did humans..." history/origin episodes.

Style DNA: off-white or paper background, clean black/graphite linework, simple editorial humans with expressive but minimal faces, muted teal/amber/red accent marks, arrows, loops, and small labels. The channel should feel like human behavior made visible through a simple system model.

Thumbnail DNA: one recognizable human/social moment or one clean loop diagram, 2-5 word emotional or contradiction label, clear arrow/loop pressure, high clarity, minimal clutter.

Forbidden drift: glossy cinematic realism as the main style, generic whiteboard stick figures, random 3D characters, dense academic diagrams, or thumbnails that look like unrelated psychology stock art.

## Renewable / Clean Energy / Storytelling Channels

### Positioning

This is NOT a renewable energy news channel, NOT an engineering lecture channel, and NOT a product review channel. This is a storytelling channel where renewable energy is simply the world and setting in which the stories happen. People do not click because they love renewable energy; they click because they love stories. Renewable energy is the setting — nothing more.

**The Golden Rule**: Stories first, technology second. Never the other way around.

Bad:
"This is a new hydrogen pipeline."

Good:
"Europe wants to spend $31 billion building something most people will never see."

**The North Star**: We are not trying to make educational videos. We are making engineering documentaries that happen to teach people something. If someone watches one of our videos and feels like they just watched a well-produced documentary rather than sat through a lesson, we have succeeded.

### Content Pillars

Every topic must clearly relate to one or more of:

*   Renewable Energy
*   Clean Energy
*   Energy Storage
*   Grid Infrastructure
*   Green Hydrogen
*   Offshore Wind
*   Solar
*   Wind
*   Battery Technology
*   AI & Energy
*   Nuclear (when connected to clean energy)
*   Energy Security
*   Future Energy Technology
*   Electricity Infrastructure
*   Energy History
*   Climate Technology (where energy is central)

Avoid becoming a generic tech, AI, engineering, or science channel. Energy must always remain the core theme.

### Every Video Must Answer A Question

The viewer must immediately understand: "What am I about to learn?"

Examples:
- Why is Europe building an island nobody will ever live on?
- Why is China stacking 35-ton blocks?
- Why are engineers trying to reinvent wind turbines after solar already won?
- Why did Australia's renewable grid struggle for four days?

The question drives the entire script.

### Pre-Writing Phase (Mandatory — Research Sheet Required)

No script may be written before research is completed. Every script begins with a Research Sheet covering:

**Main Topic**: Official project name, official company, official technology name, official location, current development status.

**Key Numbers**: Every script must contain numbers with context. Include: cost, capacity (MW/GW/MWh), height, weight, distance, efficiency, lifespan, number of workers, investment scale, number of homes powered. Numbers create anchors in the viewer's brain and dramatically improve retention. Never simply mention a number — always explain why it matters.

Bad: `100 MWh.`

Good: `100 megawatt-hours. That's enough to stabilize thousands of homes during evening demand peaks, or support heavy industrial loads for several hours depending on local demand.`

**Companies**: Always identify who built it, who funded it, who operates it, and who competes with it.

**Comparisons**: Never discuss a technology in isolation. Always compare it against: the current standard, older technology, a direct competitor, an alternative solution. Examples: Gravity storage vs lithium batteries. Small wind vs solar panels. Hydrogen pipelines vs electricity transmission. Without comparisons the audience cannot judge significance.

**Criticism**: Every topic has weaknesses. Always include them. Never create a company advertisement. Possible criticisms: cost, scaling, maintenance, economics, efficiency, politics, environmental concerns, engineering limitations, timeline delays, commercial uncertainty. This builds trust. Source company claims as company claims, never as facts.

**Latest News**: Always search recent developments before writing. Prefer the last 30–90 days. Recent news often provides better numbers, controversy, funding announcements, delays, new milestones, and criticism — these become the best retention moments.

**Official Names (Search Intent Rule)**: Always identify things properly. Do not say "a new project." Say: Princess Elisabeth Island, Aquaventus, Energy Vault EVx, AeroMINE, TESUP Atlas, Gravitricity. People search these names later, and SEO benefits.

### Audience Lessons

The audience responds better to:
*   Stories and conflict first.
*   "Bridging knowledge" using **Familiar Anchors** (see Layer 4 below).
*   Surprising projects, impossible numbers, and grid scale.
*   Altitude shifts (Building → Grid → Physics → History → Economics).
*   Skepticism balanced with optimism (balanced criticism sections).

The audience responds worse to:
*   Generic news reporting.
*   Dry, academic explanations.
*   Whiteboard/lecture styles or corporate advertisements.
*   Staying at one altitude for too long without zooming out.

### Packaging & Title Rules

Titles must create curiosity and address a specific question the viewer is about to learn.

*   *Bad*: Princess Elisabeth Island Explained
*   *Better*: Europe's €8 Billion Energy Island
*   *Better*: The Island Built Only To Move Electricity
*   *Better*: The $31 Billion Hydrogen Plan Beneath The North Sea

When a topic contains a concrete weird detail — a strange number, an impossible scale, an unexpected rejection — lead with that detail, not the broad category.

### Hook Rules

Never start with "This is...", "Today we're talking about...", or "Engineers have developed...". Instead, begin with curiosity, immediate conflict, scale, or a shocking number within the first 30 seconds.

*   *Good*: Imagine spending €8 billion on an island nobody will ever live on.
*   *Good*: For four days, one of the world's greenest electricity grids almost ran out of wind.
*   *Good*: Modern wind turbines have three blades. This one had 144.

**Passive Imagination Frame — Forbidden**: Never open with a passive imagination instruction:
*   ❌ "You probably have a picture in your head right now of a clean wind farm."
*   ❌ "Picture a massive turbine spinning in the ocean."
*   ❌ "Imagine you're inside a wind turbine tower."

These fail because the viewer is not passively imagining — they clicked because the title triggered active curiosity, suspicion, or surprise. Meet them there, not in a daydream.

For curiosity-driven or "dirty secret" titles, the viewer already suspects something is wrong. The strongest opening confirms that suspicion immediately and delivers the first reveal within the first 10 seconds:
*   ✅ "You clicked on this because the title promised a dirty secret. You're correct."
*   ✅ "Inside the base of almost every offshore wind turbine ever built sits a sealed metal cabinet. Inside that cabinet is a gas that is 23,500 times more potent than CO2."
*   ✅ "Not in a coal plant. Not in an oil refinery. Inside a wind turbine. And it leaks."

### The 30-Second Rule

The first 30 seconds determine whether YouTube keeps recommending the video.

Within the first 30 seconds include: conflict, scale, numbers, curiosity, and a real company or project. Never spend 45 seconds teaching background. The viewer should know within 30 seconds what was promised, how big the project is, what the stakes are, and who is threatened or competing.

### Story Structure

Every script follows roughly this flow:

1.  **Hook** — immediate conflict, scale, or shocking number
2.  **Conflict** — what problem is being solved or contested
3.  **Introduce Project** — official name, company, location, status
4.  **How it Works** — using Familiar Anchors (see Layer 4)
5.  **Why it Matters** — real-world significance with context on the numbers
6.  **Comparison** — against the current standard, competitor, or alternative
7.  **Criticism** — evidence-based, balanced, honest
8.  **Future** — what comes next
9.  **Ending Question** — leave the viewer with something to think about

### Retention Engine

Every 30–60 seconds, introduce something new. Possible retention devices:

- New company or new country
- New number or unexpected statistic
- New comparison or historical event
- New criticism or engineering challenge
- New project or economic challenge
- A transition that creates curiosity: "That solved one problem. But it immediately created another."
- A historical zoom-out (see Altitude Shifting below)

Never spend several minutes discussing one idea. The viewer should constantly feel: "Okay... what happens next?"

### The Competitor Formula

Follow this beat pattern to create constant forward momentum:

Problem → Company → Number → Why that number matters → Challenge → Repeat

### Layer 4: Human Context (Familiar Anchors)

This is the most important writing layer for this channel. Every major technical or engineering concept must be connected to something the viewer already knows before the formal explanation begins. This is not an analogy — it is an **anchor**. The distinction matters because analogies compare; anchors reconnect.

**Standard Anchor Mappings for This Channel**:

*   **Gravity Battery** → Physics class → "Remember the lesson where your teacher said lifting an object stores energy? This entire billion-dollar project exists because that lesson was right."
*   **Wind Turbine** → Holding your hand outside a moving car window → aerodynamic lift.
*   **Hydrogen Production** → Splitting a water molecule into hydrogen and oxygen — something viewers learned in school chemistry.
*   **AI Data Center Energy Load** → Your laptop overheating when running heavy tasks. Now imagine millions of laptops in one building.
*   **Energy Island** → An airport hub — but for electricity instead of flights.
*   **Pumped Hydro / Gravity Storage** → A dam that runs in reverse — stores energy by lifting water, releases it by letting water fall.
*   **Offshore Wind Cable** → An extension cord — just one that runs across the seabed for hundreds of kilometres.

**Before introducing any technical mechanism, ask**: "What everyday experience, school lesson, older technology, or famous historical event already teaches this?"

**Pacing and Definition Rules**:

1.  **Predict confusion**: Do not assume the viewer already understands the underlying system. Anticipate the question "Why does this work?" and resolve it immediately using a familiar anchor before moving on.
2.  **Breathing room**: Occasionally introduce calm pacing check-ins. Example: "This is actually simple." (Pause. Then the explanation.) That sentence alone relaxes the audience and signals that comprehension is within reach.
3.  **Disappear the definitions**: Never present a dry, formal definition. Melt the definition into the story. Example: "Remember high school... that's why... and that's what we call a gravity battery." The viewer absorbs the definition without feeling lectured at.

**Moments of Recognition**: Every couple of minutes the viewer should think "Oh... I know that" or "Wait... that's just like...". Those recognition moments reward the brain, reduce mental effort, and make complex engineering feel surprisingly intuitive.

### Altitude Shifting & Zooming Out

Avoid staying at one altitude (e.g., repeating Project → Company → Project → Company) for too long. Constantly shift the scale of explanation:

$$\text{Building/Machine} \rightarrow \text{Grid System} \rightarrow \text{High School Physics} \rightarrow \text{Historical Precedent} \rightarrow \text{Global Economics}$$

**Mini-Documentary Detours**: To refresh viewer attention and establish deep credibility, occasionally "zoom out" of the main project to run a 1–2 minute historical or geographic mini-documentary.

Example: When explaining China's modern gravity batteries, zoom out to tell the story of Switzerland and Italy building the first pumped hydro storage systems in the 1890s and 1907. Prove the concept has worked reliably for over a century before zooming back in to show how the modern project applies it.

Use these detours to introduce fresh visual sequences, different maps, historical archives, and timeline animations. The detour must be directly relevant — not a distraction, but a proof mechanism that gives the present-day claim historical authority.

### Human Voice Standard (Anti-Robot Writing)

Scripts must never read like they were written by a machine for a machine to narrate. They must read like a knowledgeable person telling a story to a curious friend who is not an engineer.

**The test**: Read the script aloud in your head. Does it sound like a person telling you something extraordinary? Or does it sound like a textbook section read into a microphone?

Paragraph rules for this channel:

- Paragraphs should feel like spoken language — not chopped-up AI sentences, not giant walls of text.
- Aim for 3–6 connected sentences per paragraph that flow naturally.
- Only use short punch paragraphs (1–2 sentences) when emphasising: a surprise, a reveal, a dramatic number, a sudden transition, or a question.

Example of the short punch paragraph technique in practice:

> Then the wind disappeared.
>
> For four straight days.
>
> That's exactly the scenario engineers had spent years preparing for.

Transitions must feel inevitable, not patched together. Create curiosity in the bridge itself: "That solved one problem. But it immediately created another." or "But this idea didn't actually start here."

### Identified Mistakes (Known Failure Patterns)

| Mistake | Fix |
|---|---|
| Too much explaining | Tell stories instead. Research drives structure; writing connects research into narrative. |
| Not enough numbers | Every section should introduce meaningful statistics with context. |
| No criticism | Every technology has weaknesses — discuss them. |
| No comparisons | Everything competes with something — always show what. |
| Paragraphs sounded robotic | Natural flowing speech that still leaves room for emphasis. 3–6 connected sentences, not stacked single lines. |
| Not enough official names | Always name: projects, companies, technologies, countries. |
| Research wasn't driving the script | Research drives structure. Writing simply connects the research into a narrative. |

### Thumbnail Rule

The thumbnail sells the story, not the technology. The viewer reads the thumbnail in under one second. It must communicate a tension point — not just name the subject.

**The Tension Formula**: A strong thumbnail must create a gap between what the viewer expects and what the thumbnail shows. The reader's brain fills that gap with curiosity, and curiosity drives the click.

*   *Bad*: Photo of a wind turbine. (No tension. Indistinguishable from a news article.)
*   *Better*: "SOLAR WON?" with a strange turbine. (Contradiction between text and image.)
*   *Bad*: Battery. (No tension.)
*   *Better*: "NO LITHIUM" with a giant concrete tower. (What is this? Why no lithium?)

**Number Anchoring**: When the topic contains a specific surprising number (a GWP multiplier, a cost figure, a year, a capacity), use that number as the primary thumbnail text. Numbers do more work than words at thumbnail scale because they force an immediate comparison:
*   *Weak text*: "THE LEAK" (too vague — could be any channel)
*   *Weak text*: "DIRTY WIND?" (sounds like weather)
*   *Strong text*: "23,500× WORSE" (forces the question: worse than what? worse than what I thought was clean?)
*   *Strong text*: "€8 BILLION ISLAND" (impossible scale creates instant curiosity)

**Image Direction**: The thumbnail image should make the tension visible, not just label the topic:
*   Show the hidden interior, not the clean exterior (open the turbine, not the wind farm).
*   Show the dangerous or unexpected object, not the expected object (cracked pipe and gas haze, not the spinning blade).
*   Show the contrast between expectation and reality (split-frame: clean outside / damaged inside).

Use short curiosity-driven text (2–4 words). The viewer must understand the story tension before reading the title.

### Visual Identity Lock

Default episode visual system: Cinematic Documentary System with archival-industrial documentary realism.

Identity preset name: Modern energy documentary identity.

Approved alternates and inserts:
*   Evidence Board / Case-file inserts for investigative/mystery-centered episodes.
*   Editorial diagram, map, and data inserts for mechanism, scale, comparison, or Familiar Anchor explanation sequences.
*   Hybrid Sketch System Map for historical detour mini-documentaries (e.g., zooming out to explain pumped hydro history).
*   Archive blueprints, maps, timelines, diagrams, and historical/geographical "mini-documentary" visual detours.

Style DNA: Real-world lighting, active workshop settings, clean data overlays, industrial scale, raw terrain, and historical archive imagery for detours. Avoid generic glossy future-city renders or green-energy stock imagery.

Thumbnail DNA: High-contrast, focused curiosity object (e.g., concrete blocks, massive turbine shaft), large bold text showing a tension point ("SOLAR WON?", "NO LITHIUM"), minimal clutter.

Forbidden drift: Sci-fi glow/holograms, generic corporate beauty shots, cartoon whiteboards, staying at one altitude for too long.

### Pinned Comment Standard

The pinned comment is not a thank-you message or a video summary. It is a **conversation trigger**. Its purpose is to get the viewer to reply — publicly — which increases engagement metrics and extends the comment section's life.

For this channel's content types, the strongest pinned comment formats are:

1.  **The awareness poll** (best for revelatory content): "Be honest — did you already know that [key revelation] existed, or is this the first time you're hearing about it? Drop a ✅ if you knew, or a ❌ if this completely blindsided you."
2.  **The opinion split** (best for policy or controversy content): "Do you think [company/government] is doing enough to fix this, or is this too little too late? Let's discuss below."
3.  **The stakes question** (best for future-facing content): "If [key technology/regulation] fails before [deadline], does that change how you think about [broader topic]?"
4.  **The personal experience prompt** (best for human behavior content): "Have you ever [done the thing the video reveals]? Most people have without realising it."

Never start a pinned comment with:
*   "Thanks for watching this video about..."
*   "In this video we explored..."
*   "To learn more about..."
*   Any recap sentence.

The viewer just watched the video. A summary pinned comment adds no value. A conversation-starting comment gets replies, upvotes, and keeps the section alive for weeks.

### Research Sheet Requirements

Every script must be built on a verified research sheet before writing begins. Include:

*   Official project/company/technology name.
*   Current build status and timeline.
*   Key numbers: cost (€/$), capacity (MW/GW/MWh), height, weight, distance, efficiency, lifespan, number of workers, investment scale.
*   At least one direct comparison to an existing equivalent technology or project for scale.
*   Strongest recent news (within 30-90 days if possible).
*   Criticism: every technology has weaknesses. Identify them and present them fairly. Balance optimism with evidence-based skepticism.
*   Source all company claims as company claims, not as facts.

## Human Origins / Ancient Everyday Systems

### Positioning
Use this profile for channels explaining how early humans lived, survived, and built their first cooperative networks (e.g., tools, fire, language, agriculture, early social rules).

### Strong Content Lanes
- Ancient technology: "How did early humans build X?"
- Daily life: "What did humans eat/do in the stone age?"
- Social evolution: origins of laws, relationships, rituals.
- Survival simulations: how ancient groups handled resource scarcity.

### Hook Rules

Attack the romanticized or oversimplified folk image of ancient life first. Open with a contradiction that breaks what the viewer imagines. "You probably picture ancient humans living simple, short, chaotic lives. The evidence suggests something more organized and stranger."

Never start with a geography lesson, a calendar date, or "In 200,000 BCE...". Start with a behavior, an object, or an impossible-sounding result.

**Passive Imagination Frame — Forbidden**: Never open with a passive imagination instruction:
*   ❌ "You probably picture an ancient tribe sitting around a fire."
*   ❌ "Imagine you are hunting a mammoth in the ice age."
*   ❌ "Close your eyes and think about what early humans did all day."

These fail because the viewer clicked for a concrete, evidence-backed story. Lead with the material artifact, fossil, or settlement finding immediately.

### Script Shape

1. Hook: attack the folk image of ancient life.
2. Evidence object or situation: the artifact, settlement, or behavior that breaks the assumption.
3. The system underneath: why did early humans organize this way?
4. Historical escalation: how did this system evolve or collapse?
5. Modern echo: what did this old system become?
6. Payoff: what this tells us about human nature now.

### Style DNA
Hybrid Sketch System Map, ancient/system-map identity. Off-white notebook paper, graphite or ink lines, simple period-appropriate human shapes, tool/object anchors, small map labels showing migration/territory.

## Forgotten Invention / Mystery History

### Positioning
Exposing lost, ignored, or mysterious inventions and historical episodes where the viewer acts as an investigator assembling a case file.

### Strong Content Lanes
- Suppressed or ignored patents.
- Inventions built before their time.
- Historic disappearances of technologies or inventors.
- Unsolved design anomalies.

### Hook Rules

Open with a disappearance, a missing record, a surviving artifact with no good explanation, or a result that does not match the official story. "The invention worked. The patent exists. And then nothing."

Never start with a biography introduction or a date summary. Start with the contradiction or the mystery.

**Passive Imagination Frame — Forbidden**: Never open with a passive imagination instruction:
*   ❌ "Imagine being an inventor in 1890 working in a dusty shed."
*   ❌ "You probably picture a secret vault hidden under the capital."
*   ❌ "Think of a machine that could power the world."

The viewer is in investigative mode; drop them straight into the clue, patent archive, or missing record.

### Style DNA
Evidence Board / Polaroid System, case-file mystery identity. Polaroid photos, pinned notes, red string connections used sparingly, dim investigative lighting, focused clue objects.

## Science Explainer

### Positioning
Explaining physical laws, biology, and chemistry through thought experiments, paradoxes, and clean visual mechanisms.

### Strong Content Lanes
- Physics paradoxes and thought experiments.
- Visualizing microscopic and molecular mechanisms.
- Historical lab breakthrough recreations.
- Extreme scale comparisons.

### Hook Rules

Open with an impossible-sounding measurement, a result that defies common sense, or a real experiment that contradicts what school taught. Never start with a definition. Let the viewer ask "how is that even possible?" before the answer appears.

Bad: "Today we're explaining quantum entanglement."
Better: "Two particles, 1,400 kilometers apart. One changes. The other changes instantly. No signal. No connection. Just... change."

**Passive Imagination Frame — Forbidden**: Never open with a passive imagination instruction:
*   ❌ "Imagine you are a particle traveling at the speed of light."
*   ❌ "Picture a molecule bouncing around inside a container."
*   ❌ "Think of the universe as a giant cosmic clock."

Establish the physical parameters, the paradox, or the experiment scale immediately. Let the visual engine illustrate the concept.

### Familiar Anchor Requirement

For every physics or chemistry concept, connect it to something the viewer has directly experienced. Use school experiments, household objects, body sensations, or the behavior of water, light, heat, or pressure as bridges. The viewer should not feel they are learning something alien—they should feel they already half-knew it.

### Script Shape

1. Hook: impossible result or paradox.
2. Wrong answer: the folk explanation or school version.
3. The real mechanism: explained through a Familiar Anchor first.
4. Proof: the experiment or measurement.
5. Scale shift: what this principle means at human scale vs. extreme scale.
6. Payoff: what this changes about how you see the world.

### Style DNA
Editorial Diagram System, science mechanism identity. Clean apparatus layouts, labeled forces, precise arrows, cool neutral palette, single highlighted experiment object.

## Business / Company / Market Story

### Positioning
Explaining corporate decisions, market shifts, and competitive strategies under intense pressure.

### Strong Content Lanes
- Famous corporate rivalries and wars.
- Startup bets and high-risk product launches.
- Supply chain logistics bottlenecks.
- Deconstruction of business models.

### Hook Rules

Open with a decision under pressure: a bet, a collapse risk, a hidden cost, or a competitor that should have won. Never start with a company history or founding story. Start with the moment when everything was at stake.

Bad: "Tesla was founded in 2003 by..."
Better: "In 2008, Tesla had $20 million left and one car that still didn't work."

**Passive Imagination Frame — Forbidden**: Never open with a passive imagination instruction:
*   ❌ "Picture yourself as a CEO sitting in a high-rise boardroom."
*   ❌ "Imagine you are buying a share of a new company."
*   ❌ "You probably think of a typical Wall Street broker when you..."

Lead with the concrete number, the financial threat, or the active strategic conflict.

### Script Shape

1. Hook: decision, bet, or pressure at peak.
2. Setup: what the company or product promised.
3. Escalation: what went wrong, who competed, what it cost.
4. The hidden rule: the market, incentive, or system that shaped the outcome.
5. Resolution: what actually happened.
6. Payoff: what this tells us about how markets work or companies fail.

### Style DNA
Cinematic Documentary System, business pressure identity. Offices, factory floors, warehouses, investor slide decks, financial charts, tense naturalistic lighting.

### Strong Content Lanes

**Passive Imagination Frame — Forbidden**: Never open with a passive imagination instruction:
*   ❌ "Imagine unboxing a brand new laptop."

## Data-Heavy Geopolitics / Markets

### Positioning
Visualizing global resources, borders, migration, and supply chain pressure points.

### Strong Content Lanes
- Geopolitical chokepoints.
- Global resource distribution and dependencies.
- Territorial and boundary dispute mechanics.

### Hook Rules

Open with a consequence, a chokepoint number, or a dependence the viewer didn't realize they had. "Right now, 90% of the world's advanced chip manufacturing happens within 80 miles of the Taiwan Strait." Scale and consequence first.

### Style DNA
Editorial Diagram System, data/map systems identity. Clean regional maps, highlighted routes, limited color palette, human or institutional nodes with movement arrows.
