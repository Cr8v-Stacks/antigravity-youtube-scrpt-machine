# Channel Profiles

Use these as priority examples and active operating profiles. Do not treat them as the only channels this skill can support.

## Human Behavior / System-Explanation Channels

### Positioning

Use this profile for channels that explain why humans behave the way they do through origins, effects, invisible systems, incentives, and simple visual models.

Useful short positioning:

- Why humans behave the way they do, explained simply.
- Human behavior explained through origins, effects, and invisible systems.
- A visual encyclopedia of human behavior using simple system models.

This is not self-help, motivation, generic psychology facts, or abstract theory for its own sake. It should feel like reality explained as a simple system diagram.

### Strong Content Lanes

- Human origin questions: "Why do humans...", "What did ancient humans..."
- System curiology: hidden loops behind status, money, social media, inequality, decisions.
- Weird human effects: named psychology effects and biases.
- What-if human behavior simulations.

### Winning Formats

- "Why do humans..." videos.
- "What did humans..." videos.
- "The ___ Effect" videos.
- "What if humans..." videos.
- "The system that..." videos, when the title remains understandable.

### Title Rule

Titles should feel like a question about human nature, a strange truth, or a named effect. Avoid abstract system jargon in the title even if the video uses systems thinking inside.

Better:

- Why Humans Are Obsessed With Status.
- Why Do Humans Follow Crowds?
- What Did Ancient Humans Do All Day?
- The Effect That Explains Why People Copy Each Other.

Weaker:

- The Incentive Loop Of Modern Society.
- Systemic Status Behavior Model.

### Script Shape

For standard long-form human-behavior/system-explanation videos, usually 8:00-9:30 unless the research and outline honestly support a longer runtime:

1. Hook: attack the viewer's likely wrong answer, then open the deeper question.
2. Setup: why the behavior matters.
3. Simple human model: 2-3 layers.
4. System reveal: hidden loop, incentive, or evolutionary explanation.
5. Modern manifestation: social media, money, work, dating, status, attention, or culture.
6. Final twist: a reflective or unsettling conclusion.

For "What did humans..." or origin-question videos, do not merely restate the title question. Identify the answer viewers probably brought with them, break it fast, then show the older or stranger system underneath.

### Visual Identity

Use a layered visual language:

- Editorial diagram humans as the primary identity.
- Notebook sketch style for deep thinking and origin explanations.
- Minimal expressive cartoon humans for emotional/social moments.

Avoid default stick-figure explainer and generic whiteboard style unless the user specifically wants them.

### Visual Identity Lock

Default episode visual system: Editorial Diagram System for most behavior/system videos.

Identity preset name: Human behavior loop identity.

Approved alternates and inserts:

- Social Comic System for social pressure, embarrassment, status, group reaction, and dialogue moments.
- Notebook Sketch System for reflective theory, origin thinking, and deep system breakdowns.
- Hybrid Sketch System Map for "What did humans..." history/origin episodes.

Style DNA: off-white or paper background, clean black/graphite linework, simple editorial humans with expressive but minimal faces, muted teal/amber/red accent marks, arrows, loops, and small labels. The channel should feel like human behavior made visible through a simple system model.

Thumbnail DNA: one recognizable human/social moment or one clean loop diagram, 2-5 word emotional or contradiction label, clear arrow/loop pressure, high clarity, minimal clutter.

Forbidden drift: glossy cinematic realism as the main style, generic whiteboard stick figures, random 3D characters, dense academic diagrams, or thumbnails that look like unrelated psychology stock art.

## Renewable / Clean Energy / Energy-History Channels

### Positioning

This profile is primarily a hidden-stories-from-the-history-of-energy channel. Renewable energy and clean energy are the lens, but the content engine is historical curiosity: forgotten inventors, strange machines, rejected breakthroughs, impossible numbers, early experiments, infrastructure surprises, and technologies that arrived before the world was ready.

Topics can include solar, wind, green hydrogen, battery technology, energy storage, renewable infrastructure, energy security, AI and energy, electric vehicles, clean energy history, and future energy technologies.

Do not let it become a generic electricity or technology channel. Every topic must clearly connect to energy, clean energy, renewable energy, energy infrastructure, or the energy transition.

### Audience Lessons

The audience responds better to:

- Curiosity.
- Contrarian facts.
- Surprising history.
- Hidden stories.
- Big projects.
- Infrastructure.
- Technology disruption.
- Geopolitics.
- Hidden stories from energy history.
- Historically centered technology stories.

The audience responds worse to:

- Generic news reporting.
- Dry explanations.
- Educational lectures.
- Industry updates without tension.

### Packaging Rule

Lead with the surprising thing, not the category.

When a story has a weird number, contradiction, rejection, person, date, or impossible-looking detail, make that the title and thumbnail anchor. For history Shorts, the weirdest number often wins.

Bad: "The Giant [Technology] That Shocked [Place]."

Better:

- This [Technology] Had [Weird Number].
- The [Machine] That [Surprising Outcome].
- In [Year], This Person Built [Unexpected System].
- This [Weird Number]-Part Machine [Surprising Result].

The viewer should immediately compare the hook to what they already know. Numbers work best when they create an instant mental contrast with the modern or familiar version.

For history Shorts, identify the sticky number pair:

- Date + first/forgotten/surprising outcome.
- Weird measurement + real-world result.
- Odd quantity + unexpected durability.
- Early invention + delayed adoption.

Build the Short around the number because it becomes the viewer's memory handle.

### Hook Rules

Never start with the technology. Never start with definitions. Never start by teaching.

Start with conflict, surprise, contradiction, mystery, or consequence.

For historically centered energy Shorts, use the strongest curiosity object in the first two lines:

- Modern expectation vs strange historical fact.
- Familiar technology vs absurd early version.
- Working invention vs world not ready for it.
- Private experiment vs future industry.
- Clean-energy idea vs system that could not yet adopt it.

Example:

"Modern [familiar technology] usually has [normal number].

This one had [weird number].

And it [surprising result].

Somehow, it actually worked."

After the opening shock, move quickly to the real conflict. Do not wait until the final third to reveal the system problem. The viewer should start wondering early: "If this worked, why did everyone ignore it?"

Bad: "[Technology] may replace [older technology]."

Better: "The [industry] may have a [specific resource, cost, scale, or physics] problem."

Bad: "[Technology] existed before [competing technology]."

Better: "The first successful [category] were not powered by what people assume."

### Research Requirements

Energy scripts should use real specifics:

- Companies.
- Projects.
- Numbers.
- Dates.
- Locations.
- Timelines.
- Comparisons.
- Technical claims grounded in sources.

Browse or verify current details when needed.

For videos centered on a historical person, research the person's visual appearance enough to create a reusable primary character prompt. Keep it historically grounded and avoid copying a specific copyrighted portrait or photo.

### Story Structure

For Shorts:

1. Hook.
2. Setup.
3. Tension.
4. Payoff.
5. Present-day connection.

The tension cannot be "this existed" by itself. Find the conflict:

- It worked, but nobody followed it.
- It was too early for the grid, market, or public.
- The inventor solved one problem but hit a bigger system barrier.
- The technology looked ridiculous but pointed toward the future.
- America saw the idea and ignored it.
- A famous rival, competing company, or industry shift made the invention more interesting than the machine alone.

The ending should not ramble. It should land one concrete idea:

- The machine worked, but the market moved elsewhere.
- The inventor was already part of a bigger technology race.
- The invention proved something, but failed to become the winning system.
- The viewer realizes the story was not just about the device, but about timing, competition, or adoption.

Avoid ending with broad meaning if a sharper conflict exists. A strong ending names the hidden villain: the market, the grid, standardization, cost, public fear, industry incentives, government timing, or lack of demand.

For long-form:

1. Tension-first opening.
2. Specific actor or project.
3. Stakes and numbers.
4. Obstacle.
5. Comparison or historical context.
6. Consequence.
7. Present/future implication.

### History Rule

Do not merely tell what happened. Tell why the story is surprising.

Wrong: "[Person] built the first [technology]."

Better: "[Person] built the first [technology], and the people around it rejected, ignored, misunderstood, or failed to scale it."

The surprise is the story, not the technology.

### Thumbnail Rule

Do not focus on the technology alone. Focus on curiosity.

Prefer the strongest curiosity element:

- Weird number: "[WEIRD NUMBER]"
- Contradiction: "IT WORKED"
- Rejection: "REJECTED"
- Private scale: "[PRIVATE / UNEXPECTED SYSTEM]"
- Outcome: "[SURPRISING RESULT]"

Use these as patterns, not fixed phrases. The thumbnail should use the most concrete curiosity object available for the current topic.

Bad: plain [technology] image.

Better: a rejection, number, contradiction, cost, danger, or outcome with the technology as visual support.

Bad: plain [object] image.

Better: the specific problem, number, conflict, or consequence with the object supporting the message.

### Visual Identity Lock

Default episode visual system: Cinematic Documentary System with archival-industrial documentary realism.

Identity preset name: Archival-industrial identity.

Approved alternates and inserts:

- Evidence Board / Polaroid System for investigation, forgotten history, and hidden-connection episodes.
- Archive artifact, evidence/document, map/location, timeline, blueprint, patent, and data/diagram inserts inside the cinematic identity.
- Editorial diagram inserts only when a mechanism, grid constraint, cost curve, or comparison must be simplified.

Style DNA: real-world lighting, grounded workshops, labs, factories, historical rooms, patents, blueprints, machines, infrastructure, hands interacting with objects, period-specific props, naturalistic warm/cool shadows, no sci-fi gloss.

Thumbnail DNA: one physical machine, inventor, blueprint, project, or infrastructure object tied to a weird number, rejection, cost, impossible result, or system obstacle. Text should be short and concrete.

Forbidden drift: generic green-energy stock photos, glossy future-city renders, random solar/wind beauty shots without conflict, fake logos, neon holograms, or purely abstract climate imagery.

## Human Origins / Ancient Everyday Systems

### Positioning
Use this profile for channels explaining how early humans lived, survived, and built their first cooperative networks (e.g., tools, fire, language, agriculture, early social rules).

### Strong Content Lanes
- Ancient technology: "How did early humans build X?"
- Daily life: "What did humans eat/do in the stone age?"
- Social evolution: origins of laws, relationships, rituals.
- Survival simulations: how ancient groups handled resource scarcity.

### Style DNA
Hybrid Sketch System Map, ancient/system-map identity. Off-white notebook paper, graphite or ink lines, simple period-appropriate human shapes, tool/object anchors, small map labels showing migration/territory.

## Forgotten Invention / Mystery History

### Positioning
Exposing lost, ignored, or mysterious inventions and historical episodes where the viewer acts as an investigator assembling a case file.

### Strong Content Lanes
- Suppressed or ignored patents.
- Inventions built before their time.
- Historic disappearances of technologies or inventors.
- Unsolved design anomalies.

### Style DNA
Evidence Board / Polaroid System, case-file mystery identity. Polaroid photos, pinned notes, red string connections used sparingly, dim investigative lighting, focused clue objects.

## Science Explainer

### Positioning
Explaining physical laws, biology, and chemistry through thought experiments, paradoxes, and clean visual mechanisms.

### Strong Content Lanes
- Physics paradoxes and thought experiments.
- Visualizing microscopic and molecular mechanisms.
- Historical lab breakthrough recreations.
- Extreme scale comparisons.

### Style DNA
Editorial Diagram System, science mechanism identity. Clean apparatus layouts, labeled forces, precise arrows, cool neutral palette, single highlighted experiment object.

## Business / Company / Market Story

### Positioning
Explaining corporate decisions, market shifts, and competitive strategies under intense pressure.

### Strong Content Lanes
- Famous corporate rivalries and wars.
- Startup bets and high-risk product launches.
- Supply chain logistics bottlenecks.
- Deconstruction of business models.

### Style DNA
Cinematic Documentary System, business pressure identity. Offices, factory floors, warehouses, investor slide decks, financial charts, tense naturalistic lighting.

## Tech Product / Product Comparison

### Positioning
Auditing product performance and comparing specifications against real-world user constraints.

### Strong Content Lanes
- Spec-sheet vs reality comparisons.
- Durability and thermal performance testing.
- Design trade-offs.

### Style DNA
Cinematic Documentary System, tech product identity. Inspectable hardware close-ups, gloved hands, test benches, clean comparative labels, no fake brand logos.

## Ancient History / Economic History

### Positioning
Tracing the evolution of commerce, money, debt, inflation, and imperial finance.

### Strong Content Lanes
- Origin of coinage and minting.
- Trade route economics.
- Historic hyperinflation events.
- Taxation and resource extraction systems.

### Style DNA
Hybrid Sketch System Map, economic flow map identity. Paper texture, maps, coins, ledgers, simple period figures, flow arrows, timeline markers.

## Data-Heavy Geopolitics / Markets

### Positioning
Visualizing global resources, borders, migration, and supply chain pressure points.

### Strong Content Lanes
- Geopolitical chokepoints.
- Global resource distribution and dependencies.
- Territorial and boundary dispute mechanics.

### Style DNA
Editorial Diagram System, data/map systems identity. Clean regional maps, highlighted routes, limited color palette, human or institutional nodes with movement arrows.
